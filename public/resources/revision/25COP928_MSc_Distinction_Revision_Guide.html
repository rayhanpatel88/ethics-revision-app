Professionalism, Ethics & Codes of Conduct

The foundation: why professional ethics is not optional, and what it demands of you.

A · Core Definitions

B · Why It Matters

Computing professionals exercise asymmetric power over society. They design systems that affect healthcare, finance, elections, and personal safety. Unlike earlier professions, there is often no patient or client in the room — the "users" are abstract masses, and the harms are invisible until catastrophic. The BCS and ACM codes exist precisely to internalise ethical accountability in the absence of a direct relationship.

C · The Four Ethical Theories — Applied to Cyber
Utilitarianism
  • Greatest good for greatest number
  • Outcomes-focused
  • Thinker: Bentham / Mill
  • Cyber: delay vulnerable app release to prevent mass harm
  • Weakness: can justify oppressing a minority for majority benefit
Deontology
  • Rules and duties above outcomes
  • Categorical Imperative (Kant)
  • Act only as you'd will universalised
  • Cyber: never release insecure code regardless of profit gain
  • Weakness: rigid; ignores consequences
Virtue Ethics
  • Character over rules or outcomes
  • Thinker: Aristotle
  • What would a person of good character do?
  • Cyber: integrity, honesty, courage in whistleblowing
  • Weakness: unclear in novel dilemmas
Consequentialism
  • Broader than utilitarianism
  • Any theory judging acts by results
  • Includes negative consequences
  • Cyber: evaluate full impact chain of AI deployment
  • Weakness: requires perfect prediction of outcomes

E · Frances Haugen — The Definitive Whistleblowing Case

Frances Haugen — Facebook / Meta Whistleblower (2021)

Professionalism · Whistleblowing · Corporate Ethics
What Happened
Former product manager on Facebook's civic integrity team disclosed thousands of internal documents to Congress and the WSJ, revealing Facebook knowingly optimised engagement algorithms over user safety, exacerbating political polarisation, youth mental health damage, and misinformation spread.
Ethical Tension
Corporate profit vs. public safety. The documents showed internal research confirming harm, yet the company chose not to act. This is a textbook tension between consequentialist ethics (harm minimisation) and corporate deontology (fiduciary duty to shareholders).
BCS/ACM Application
Both codes demand that professionals prioritise public interest above employer instruction. Haugen's act — first raising internally, then escalating externally — mirrors the correct BCS escalation protocol. Her actions were ethically justified under both codes.
Professional Lesson
Algorithmic responsibility is not delegable. If you design, deploy, or maintain a system causing societal harm, professional ethics demand intervention — regardless of corporate hierarchy.
Exam Angle
Use as primary evidence for: platform accountability, profit vs. safety dilemmas, whistleblowing escalation, and the limits of compliance-only ethics.
F · The Whistleblowing Escalation Ladder
1
Internal — Document & Build Evidence
Compile factual evidence of the ethical or legal violation. Do not act alone without documentation. Recruit allies where possible.
2
Internal — Official Reporting Channels
Use ethics hotlines, compliance officers, senior management, or board-level reporting. Document all responses and inaction.
3
Regulatory — Prescribed External Bodies
Report to the ICO (data), FCA (finance), NCSC (security), or relevant professional body (BCS). Protected Disclosures Act provides legal protection in UK.
4

Only after all above exhausted and ongoing serious public harm. Haugen reached this stage correctly. Not a first-response action.

🎓 Distinction-Level Talking Points
  • Legal compliance is a floor, not a ceiling. Facebook's ad targeting practices may have been technically lawful while being profoundly unethical.
  • The concept of "algorithmic harm" — that automated systems can cause discrimination and damage without direct human intent — challenges traditional models of legal liability and professional responsibility.
  • Professional codes are aspirational and enforceable: BCS can expel members; ACM can sanction. This creates a form of professional governance that operates above mere legal compliance.
  • The "public interest test": an action is professionally defensible if a reasonable fellow-professional, seeing all the facts, would consider it to be in the public interest — a standard borrowed from journalism and medicine.
🎯 Exam Insight — What Examiners Want

Examiners want you to demonstrate that you understand ethics as a practice, not a theory. Show that you can apply a theory (e.g., deontology), then critically evaluate its limits (e.g., it fails when rules conflict), then pivot to a professional code (e.g., BCS public interest principle), then land on a real-world example (Haugen). This is the distinction-level answer architecture.

Privacy as a fundamental right — and the legal frameworks designed to protect it.

A · Core Definitions

C · The 7 UK GDPR Principles (Article 5)

P1 — Lawfulness, Fairness & Transparency

Processing must have a valid lawful basis; individuals must be informed in plain language what is done with their data.

P2 — Purpose Limitation

Data collected for a specific purpose cannot be reused for incompatible purposes without fresh lawful basis.

P3 — Data Minimisation

Only collect what is adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary. No "just in case" collection.

P4 — Accuracy

Personal data must be accurate and kept up to date. Inaccurate data must be corrected or erased promptly.

P5 — Storage Limitation

Data must not be held longer than necessary. Retention schedules are mandatory, not optional.

P6 — Integrity & Confidentiality

Technical and organisational security measures must protect against unauthorised access, loss, or destruction.

P7 — Accountability (The Master Principle)

The data controller must be able to demonstrate compliance with all of the above. This transforms GDPR from a rule-following exercise into an active governance obligation. Accountability is why a DPO, audit trails, and privacy-by-design exist.

D · Real-World Cases — Data Privacy Failures

Meta / Facebook

Cambridge Analytica: 87 million profiles harvested without valid consent via a third-party app. Violates P1 (lawfulness), P2 (purpose), P3 (minimisation). £500k ICO fine (pre-GDPR maximum). Under GDPR: potentially £1.3 billion.

TikTok

CEO testimony revealed collection of keystroke patterns, device IDs, and cross-site tracking far exceeding stated purpose. ICO fined TikTok £12.7M (2023) for processing children's data without valid consent or lawful basis.

OpenAI / ChatGPT

GDPR investigations in multiple EU states regarding training data collection without consent, right to erasure difficulties, and inaccurate outputs about real individuals. Italy temporarily banned the service (2023).

TEMU

Privacy investigations into the app's data collection practices, including alleged excessive permissions and data transfers to servers in China, raising both GDPR and national security concerns.

Oracle Health Breach (2025)

Patient data breach affecting healthcare providers; highlights Controller vs. Processor liability — Oracle as a cloud health data processor bears direct accountability obligations under UK GDPR post-DPA 2018.

Gambling Firms / Meta Data Sharing

Gambling platforms sharing customer data with Facebook via pixel tracking without transparent disclosure. Violates P1 (fairness/transparency) and P2 (purpose limitation). ICO enforcement ongoing.

E · OSINT, Digital Footprints & Ethical Implications

Open Source Intelligence (OSINT) involves aggregating publicly available information to build profiles of individuals. In seminar exercises, students discover how much information is derivable from social media, domain registrations, photo metadata, and public records. This raises a crucial distinction: legality vs. ethics.

⚠️ The Aggregation Problem

Each individual piece of public data may be harmless and legally accessible. But when aggregated, the profile becomes an invasion of privacy — knowing someone's name (public), workplace (LinkedIn), daily gym time (Strava), and home neighbourhood (photo metadata) together creates a surveillance capability that no single piece would enable. This is why the Strava heatmap revealed military base locations, and why OSINT is ethically complex even when technically lawful. GDPR's "identifiable natural person" definition is designed to address this aggregation risk.

F · AI & Data Privacy — Emerging Tensions

AI Training Data

Large language models trained on scraped internet data may incorporate personal information without consent or awareness. Does training on publicly posted text constitute "processing personal data"? Most DPAs say yes.

Algorithmic Profiling

Article 22 GDPR creates rights against solely automated decisions with significant effects. AI credit scoring, insurance pricing, and content curation all engage this right — but "solely automated" is often contested.

Children's Privacy

Children require heightened protection — Art. 8 sets 13-16 as the age of digital consent. TikTok's £12.7M ICO fine was specifically for failing to protect children's data. Age verification is technically and ethically contentious.

Biometric Data

Facial recognition at workplaces and public spaces processes special category data requiring explicit consent or substantial public interest. Its use in policing remains legally contested in the UK.

🎓 Distinction-Level Talking Points — Data Privacy
  • Privacy as power asymmetry: Data collection is fundamentally about one party (organisation) accumulating knowledge about another (individual) without reciprocal transparency. GDPR attempts to redress this power imbalance structurally.
  • Data commodification: The surveillance capitalism model (Zuboff) treats personal data as raw material for behavioural prediction products. GDPR's "lawful basis" requirement is a direct challenge to this model.
  • The consent fiction: Consent under GDPR is theoretically meaningful, but cookie banners and 50-page privacy policies make genuine informed consent practically impossible. This is a structural governance failure, not merely a compliance failure.
  • Cross-border data flows: The Schrems II ruling invalidated the EU-US Privacy Shield, establishing that US surveillance law makes meaningful adequacy decisions impossible. The new UK-US data bridge remains contested.

§ SECTION 03

Intellectual Property in Computing

How law constructs artificial monopolies to incentivise innovation — and the tensions this creates.

A · Why IP Exists — The Governance Rationale

Intellectual property law solves a market failure: knowledge goods are non-rival and non-excludable — once a piece of code or invention is shared, it can be copied at near-zero cost, eliminating the creator's ability to recoup investment. IP law creates temporary, legally enforceable monopolies to incentivise innovation and creative production. The tension is systemic: monopolies restrict access, raise prices, and can paradoxically retard innovation if over-extended.

D · Key Legal Cases

Infopaq v Danske Dagblades (2009)

ECJ ruling: reproducing as few as 11 words constitutes copyright infringement if the extract reflects the "author's own intellectual creation." Sets the originality threshold — genuine intellectual choices, not mechanical production, attract protection. Critical for software copyright and AI-generated content debates.

Aerotel / Macrossan (2006)

Court of Appeal established the four-step patent test for software: (1) construe the claim; (2) identify actual contribution; (3) check technical nature; (4) check excluded matter. Software is only patentable if it provides a technical solution to a technical problem going beyond normal program execution.

Blyth v Birmingham Waterworks (1856)

The reasonableness standard for negligence — what a prudent and reasonable person would do. Now applied to AI liability: AI systems (and their designers) are assessed against what a reasonable, competent professional would have done. Links IP/ethics to professional responsibility.

Whelan v Jaslow (1986)

US case establishing that non-literal elements of software (structure, sequence, organisation) can be protected by copyright — not just literal code. Extended protection beyond simple verbatim copying to architecture-level similarity.

E · AI & IP — The Frontier Tensions
⚠️ High-Stakes Emerging Issue — AI Training & Copyright
  • Training data legality: AI models trained on scraped internet content may incorporate copyrighted text, code, and art without licence. Getty Images v Stability AI and The New York Times v OpenAI represent the leading edge of this litigation.
  • Generated outputs: If an AI generates content substantially similar to a protected work in its training data, this may constitute infringement — but who is liable? The model? The user? The developer?
  • Synthetic content ownership: Current UK law does not grant copyright to AI-generated works unless a human "author" made substantial creative choices. The CDPA 1988 s.9(3) "computer-generated works" provision attributes copyright to the person who made the arrangements — but this was designed for automated generation, not LLM-scale creation.
  • The anti-commons problem: Over-propertisation of IP can create a "tragedy of the anti-commons" — too many rights holders veto further innovation. Open source licensing exists partly to counteract this.

🎓 Distinction-Level Talking Points — IP
  • The anti-commons paradox: IP law creates monopolies to incentivise production, but excessive IP can prevent the follow-on innovation that the system was designed to promote. Patent thickets in software are a well-documented example.
  • Scraping and T&Cs: Contractual restrictions on data scraping (via Terms of Service) are legally distinct from IP rights. Breach of ToS is contract law, not copyright — but both can restrict OSINT and AI training.
  • The Infopaq threshold in an AI world: If even 11 words of human-authored text attract copyright, every RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation) output that cites source material risks infringement. This will be the defining IP litigation of the next decade.

§ SECTION 04

Information Governance

How organisations tame the chaos of data — and why failure costs far more than compliance.

A · Core Definition

Information Governance (IG) is the overarching framework of policies, processes, and accountabilities through which an organisation manages the entire lifecycle of its information assets — from creation to disposal — in a way that is legally compliant, operationally sound, and strategically valuable. IG is broader than data protection: it encompasses all organisational information, not just personal data.

B · Why IG Matters — The Four Failure Modes

Legal Risk

Failure to comply with GDPR, DPA 2018, the CMA, or sector-specific regulations (NHS, financial services) results in ICO fines up to £17.5M, regulatory sanctions, and potential criminal liability for individuals.

Reputational Risk

Data breaches destroy public trust. TikTok, Post Office, and NHS data scandals demonstrate that governance failures become headline news — with long-tail reputational damage that outlasts the legal penalty.

Operational Risk

Poor data quality and governance directly impairs operational decision-making. The Post Office Horizon scandal shows how accepting inaccurate data as truth caused catastrophic real-world harm — hundreds of wrongful prosecutions.

Strategic Risk

Organisations that cannot govern their data cannot leverage it for AI, analytics, or competitive intelligence — creating a direct link between IG maturity and strategic capability.

D · Key Case Studies — Governance Failures

The Post Office Horizon IT Scandal

Information Governance · Data Accuracy · Accountability
What Happened
Faulty Fujitsu Horizon accounting software generated erroneous shortfall reports. The Post Office accepted this data as definitive, resulting in the wrongful prosecution of over 900 subpostmasters for theft and false accounting between 2000–2014.
IG Failure
Catastrophic violation of GDPR Principle 4 (Accuracy) and Principle 7 (Accountability). The organisation treated flawed system data as ground truth without verification. No effective audit trail; no challenge mechanism; governance structures captured by commercial interest.
Accountability Gap
Neither Fujitsu nor Post Office leadership faced prosecution. This illustrates the "accountability diffusion" problem — when multiple parties share governance responsibility, none may face consequence. WHO is the data controller? Who holds ultimate accountability?
Professional Lesson
Computing professionals have a duty to flag when system outputs are being used as evidence without adequate validation. Silence in the face of known system flaws is an ethical failure, not a professional position.

NHS AI Data Access & DeepMind / Palantir Controversies

Public Sector · AI Ethics · Information Governance
Context
Royal Free NHS Trust shared 1.6 million identifiable patient records with DeepMind (Google subsidiary) without proper transparency or lawful basis — ICO found this in breach of data protection law. Later, NHS contracts with Palantir for federated data platform raised concerns about commercial access to the UK's most sensitive health dataset.
Core Tension
Genuine public benefit (AI-accelerated diagnostics, pandemic response) vs. patient privacy rights and risks of commercial exploitation of NHS data. This is the archetypal innovation vs. privacy governance dilemma.
Governance Failures
Inadequate transparency with patients; failure to establish appropriate data processor agreements; insufficient oversight of commercial partners' use of data.
Professional Lesson
Public sector IG must balance innovation against the higher duty of care owed to citizens who have no choice but to engage with public services. Commercial partnership does not transfer governance responsibility.

Distinguish between technical compliance and substantive governance — an organisation can have a DPO and a retention policy while still failing catastrophically at governance (as the Post Office demonstrates).

D · Real Attacks — The Attack Lifecycle Applied

Bybit Cryptocurrency Heist (February 2025)

CMA S.1/S.3 · Nation-State Attribution · Crypto Crime
What Happened
One of the largest cryptocurrency thefts in history — approximately $1.5 billion stolen from Bybit exchange. Attributed to the Lazarus Group (DPRK-affiliated). Attack compromised multi-signature cold wallet infrastructure through a sophisticated UI manipulation attack on a third-party signing interface (Safe{Wallet}).
Attack Stages
Recon (mapping Bybit's cold wallet infrastructure) → Scanning (identifying Safe{Wallet} dependency) → Gaining Access (compromising Safe{Wallet} developer environment — supply chain attack) → Maintaining Access (implanting malicious JavaScript) → Execution (manipulating UI during a routine transaction to redirect funds).
Legal Analysis
Multiple CMA 1990 offences: S.1 (unauthorised access to Safe{Wallet} systems), S.3 (unauthorised modification of transaction data), potentially S.3ZA if deemed to threaten financial system integrity. Cross-jurisdictional enforcement against nation-state actors remains practically impossible.
Professional Lesson
Supply chain attacks now dominate sophisticated threat actor playbooks. Governance of third-party software dependencies is as critical as internal security controls. The weakness was not in Bybit's systems but in its supply chain — an IG and cyber governance failure simultaneously.

⚠️ Critical Legal Gap — Is The CMA Fit For AI?

The CMA was written in 1990 for a world of human hackers breaking into discrete systems. It struggles with: (1) AI-automated attacks — who has "intent" when an AI system autonomously discovers and exploits a vulnerability? (2) Adversarial AI — injecting malicious inputs into AI models; does this "modify" computer material within S.3? (3) Nation-state cyber warfare — the Act is extraterritorial but practically unenforceable against foreign state actors. This is a key distinction-level argument: the legal framework has not kept pace with the threat landscape.

  • Cyber resilience vs. cyber security: Security is about preventing breaches; resilience is about ensuring business continuity when breaches occur. Modern governance frameworks (NIST CSF, ISO 27001) treat both as essential — an organisation that can only prevent, not recover, is fundamentally insecure.

Red Teaming & Adversarial Thinking

Simulating the enemy to defeat them — and the profound ethical questions this raises.

A · What Is Red Teaming?

Red teaming is the practice of role-playing as a hostile actor against your own systems, processes, or people to identify vulnerabilities before real attackers do. Originally a Cold War military concept (the "Red Army" vs. the "Blue Army"), it has become central to modern cybersecurity governance, AI safety testing, and national defence strategy. The key distinction from penetration testing: red teaming is scenario-driven, adversarial-mindset simulation rather than technical vulnerability scanning — it tests people, processes, and culture, not just technology.

C · The HUHT/HULT/LUHT/LULT Actor Framework

This framework classifies threat actors along two axes: Capability (High/Low) and Desire to cause harm to the target (High/Low). Each quadrant demands a different defensive and red teaming posture.

D · Digital Twins & Attack Rehearsal

A digital twin is a virtual replica of a physical or digital system that can be used for simulation, testing, and attack rehearsal without affecting live operations. In red teaming contexts, digital twins allow teams to: (1) test attacks against realistic system models; (2) rehearse incident response; (3) identify single points of failure; and (4) model supply chain attacks without legal or operational risk. Critical national infrastructure operators increasingly use digital twins to rehearse HUHT attack scenarios against power grids, water treatment, and financial clearing systems.

E · The Ethics of Red Teaming — The Core Debate
⚠️ Ethical Tension — "Is It Ethical To Teach Offensive Techniques?"

The strongest objection: teaching red team techniques creates a dual-use knowledge problem. The same skills that enable an authorised red teamer to test a bank's defences could enable an unauthorised attacker to breach them. Arguments for teaching offensive techniques:

  • Security through understanding: Defenders who cannot think like attackers will always be reactive. Offensive skills are a prerequisite for effective defence.
  • Controlled creation: Skills taught in professional and academic contexts come with professional accountability structures (BCS codes, employment law, CMA liability) that deter misuse.
  • Counterfactual: Determined attackers can learn these techniques regardless. Refusing to teach them only disadvantages defenders.

Arguments against / limitations:

  • Not all students will be ethical: Academic institutions cannot guarantee that red team skills will only be used defensively.
  • Lowering barriers: Professionalised education does reduce the skill barrier to conducting attacks — some attacks require capabilities only available after formal training.
  • The "just following orders" analogy: Professional context does not always prevent misuse — insider threats emerge from trained professionals.

F · Legal Framework — DPA, RIPA, ACAS
⚠️ High-Score Legal Threshold — Telecommunications (Lawful Business Practice) Regulations 2000

The TLBP Regulations create the narrow business-purpose exemption within RIPA allowing monitoring without explicit consent if: (1) the interception is for a legitimate business purpose (crime detection, regulatory compliance, business transaction verification, system standards); (2) the employer has taken all reasonable steps to inform employees that monitoring may occur; (3) monitoring is proportionate to the stated purpose. Failure to satisfy all three limbs = RIPA breach = potential criminal liability for the employer, not just civil GDPR breach.

🎓 Distinction-Level Talking Points — Workplace Monitoring
  • The digital-physical surveillance merge: Traditional employment law was designed for a physical workplace. Remote working, BYOD (Bring Your Own Device), and always-on monitoring tools have dissolved the boundary between work and personal life — GDPR's "purpose limitation" principle has never been more strained.
  • Productivity surveillance vs. performance management: There is a meaningful ethical distinction between monitoring outcomes (what was produced) and monitoring process (every keystroke, mouse movement, idle second). The latter treats employees as machines and imports the logic of industrial Taylorism into knowledge work, where it is both empirically ineffective and ethically corrosive.
  • The consent problem: Employment relationships are structurally coercive — an employee who "consents" to biometric monitoring as a condition of employment has not given free consent under GDPR. This is why Art. 9 special category data (biometrics) cannot rely on employment consent — an alternative lawful basis is required.

A · The Reasonableness Standard — Legal Tradition & Moral Baseline

The "reasonable person" standard originated in Blyth v Birmingham Waterworks Co (1856) as the test for negligence — conduct is judged against what a prudent and reasonable person would have done in the same circumstances. In computing ethics, this standard has been extended to measure professional conduct, AI system decisions, and organisational liability. The question is not what the defendant intended, but what a reasonable professional would have done. This matters because it creates an objective standard for professional accountability that transcends subjective good intentions.

"The reasonableness standard asks not what you thought was right, but what any responsible professional in your position would have recognised as their duty."

The Distinction-Level Answer Architecture
1
Define & Contextualise
Open with a clear, MSc-level definition of the central concept. Immediately situate it in its broader governance / societal context. Do not spend more than 15% of your answer here.
2
Apply Multiple Frameworks
Apply at minimum two ethical theories AND a legal framework AND a professional code to the same scenario. Show how they converge OR diverge. Divergence is more intellectually interesting — lean into it.

Recurring High-Value Arguments — Use In Any Relevant Question

"Legal Compliance ≠ Ethical Conduct"

Facebook's data practices were largely legal; they were not ethical. This argument applies to any question where a party has technically complied with regulation but caused harm. Always worth deploying.

"Accountability Diffusion"

When responsibility is shared across organisations, individuals, and technical systems, no single actor bears clear accountability — enabling harm to occur unchecked. Post Office Horizon, supply chain attacks, AI decisions.

"Power Asymmetry"

Data collection, workplace surveillance, and algorithmic decision-making all involve one party accumulating knowledge and power over another. Governance frameworks exist to redress this asymmetry — ask whether they succeed.

"The Reasonableness Standard"

What would a competent, reasonable professional in this role have done? This standard can be deployed to evaluate any professional conduct question, and connects to Blyth v Birmingham Waterworks and AI liability.

"Innovation-Privacy Trade-off"

Almost every data and AI ethics question involves the same underlying tension. Acknowledge the genuine public benefit of innovation (NHS AI diagnostics, fraud detection) while articulating why it does not automatically override rights.

"The Floor/Ceiling Distinction"

Law sets a floor; ethics sets a ceiling. Professional codes demand conduct above the legal minimum. This argument allows you to analyse situations where an actor is both legally compliant and professionally deficient.

Frances Haugen — Facebook / Meta Whistleblower (2021)

Ethics Professional Conduct Platform Governance
What Happened
Former Facebook product manager leaked internal research documents to Congress and media, revealing Facebook knowingly amplified division, misinformation, and harm to youth mental health to optimise engagement metrics — prioritising profit over safety.
Stakeholders
Facebook/Meta (corporate interest); users (safety, privacy); society (democratic health); Haugen (professional duty vs. employment obligation); regulators (governance failure)
Ethical Issues
Consequentialism: net harm to society > net corporate benefit. Deontology: Facebook violated a duty of care to users. Virtue ethics: Haugen demonstrated moral courage. Professional ethics: BCS/ACM public interest obligation demands exactly this action.
Governance Failures
Internal research suppressed; no effective board-level ethics oversight; external regulatory frameworks unable to access internal algorithms; profit metrics embedded into governance structures, displacing safety metrics.

Professional Lesson
Algorithmic harm is professional harm. The engineers who built and maintained these systems bore professional responsibility — not merely legal liability. Haugen's escalation path (internal → external → media) is the BCS/ACM mandated protocol.

The Post Office Horizon IT Scandal (2000–2014)

Information Governance Data Accuracy Professional Ethics
What Happened
Faulty Fujitsu Horizon accounting software generated false shortfall figures. Post Office treated outputs as definitive, leading to the wrongful prosecution of over 900 subpostmasters for theft/false accounting. The largest miscarriage of justice in UK legal history.

Subpostmasters (victims); Post Office (institutional perpetrator); Fujitsu (software provider); government (shareholder and regulator); ICO; UK justice system

Computing professionals who know a system is producing erroneous outputs have a positive professional duty to speak — not merely to comply with instructions. Silence in the face of known system error is a BCS/ACM code violation, not professional neutrality.

CMA S.1 (unauthorised access to Safe{Wallet} systems); S.3 (unauthorised modification of transaction data). Practically unenforceable against nation-state actors. Highlights gap between legal frameworks designed for individual hackers and state-sponsored threat actors operating with impunity.
Governance Failures
Insufficient third-party security assessment (supply chain); inadequate monitoring of signing interface integrity; over-reliance on cryptographic security at the protocol level while ignoring UI-layer vulnerability; no real-time anomaly detection on transaction patterns.
Professional Lesson
Security governance must extend to supply chains. Every third-party dependency is an attack surface — and sophisticated adversaries exploit the weakest link, not the strongest. HUHT adversaries will always find the unguarded door. Supply chain security is now a Board-level governance obligation.

OpenAI & GDPR Privacy Investigations (2023–2025)

Data Privacy AI Ethics GDPR
What Happened
Multiple EU data protection authorities (Italy, France, Spain, Germany, Poland) investigated ChatGPT for: training data collection without consent; inability to honour right to erasure (Art.17) for data incorporated into model weights; inaccurate AI-generated statements about real individuals; inadequate transparency about data processing.
Core GDPR Tensions
Art.17 (Right to Erasure): once personal data is incorporated into LLM weights, it cannot be "deleted" — technically impossible without retraining the model. Art.22 (Automated Decisions): AI-generated text about individuals may constitute automated processing with significant effects. Art.5(1)(a): transparency obligations — users cannot understand how their data is used in training.
Emerging Legal Question
Does web scraping for AI training constitute "processing personal data" even if individual data points are public? Most DPAs answer yes — if the data relates to an identifiable person, GDPR applies regardless of public availability. This challenges the entire training data model of large AI companies.
Professional Lesson
AI engineers have a professional obligation to design systems capable of honouring data subject rights. Building systems structurally incapable of compliance (e.g., models that cannot forget training data) is a foreseeable GDPR failure that engineers should flag as a design requirement, not a later legal problem.

TEMU App Privacy Concerns (2023–2025)

Data Minimisation App Security National Security
Context
TEMU (PDD Holdings, China) faced privacy investigations in multiple jurisdictions. Security researchers and DPAs raised concerns about excessive app permissions, potential data exfiltration, transfer of user data to servers in jurisdictions without adequate GDPR data transfer safeguards, and opaque data sharing practices.
GDPR Analysis
P3 (Data Minimisation) — permissions extend beyond app functionality necessity. P1 (Transparency) — data sharing with parent company and third parties inadequately disclosed. International Transfer concerns — Chapter V GDPR requires adequacy, SCCs, or BCRs for data transfers to non-adequate countries. China lacks UK/EU adequacy decision.
Broader Tension
Global tech companies subject to domestic law of their country of incorporation (including China's Data Security Law, which can require disclosure to state actors) while processing EU/UK personal data subject to GDPR. This creates a structural conflict of law that the GDPR's Chapter V transfer provisions inadequately address.
Professional Lesson
Data protection and national security are not separate domains. Computing professionals must consider the entire data supply chain — including where data ultimately ends up and under whose legal authority — not merely the technical spec of their own system.

Workplace Surveillance — Amazon Warehouse Monitoring

Employment Ethics Proportionality GDPR
Context
Amazon warehouse operations use extensive real-time monitoring: scanner tracking of pick-rates, toilet break timing, idle detection systems, AI-powered camera monitoring, and in some cases wearable devices. Workers report pressure from algorithmic management systems — being "managed" by automated targets rather than human supervisors.
Ethical Issues
Proportionality: is second-by-second productivity tracking proportionate to legitimate business interests? Dignity: does reducing workers to productivity metrics dehumanise the employment relationship? Power asymmetry: workers have no ability to challenge algorithmic decisions or access the data used against them (Art.22 GDPR).
Legal Analysis
GDPR Art.22 — automated decision-making; workers have rights against being subject solely to algorithmic management with significant effects. ICO Employment Practices guidance requires proportionality. ACAS Code — monitoring must be disclosed and proportionate. Art.9 — health/biometric data from wearables requires explicit consent.
Professional Lesson
Engineers who design monitoring systems bear professional responsibility for the human consequences of those systems. Optimising for productivity metrics without considering the human cost — burnout, injury, dehumanisation — is an ethical failure regardless of legal compliance.

Vulnerability Research & Responsible Disclosure Debates

Ethical Hacking CMA S.3A Professional Responsibility
Context
Security researchers occupying a legally and ethically ambiguous space: discovering and disclosing vulnerabilities serves genuine public good (patching creates a safer internet) but may technically constitute unauthorised access (CMA S.1) or supply of hacking tools (S.3A) without explicit bug bounty authorisation.
Ethical Tensions
Full disclosure: forces vendor action but potentially arms attackers. Coordinated disclosure: best practice but dependent on vendor responsiveness. Selling to brokers: commercially rational but ethically questionable. The CVE/NVD system relies on responsible researchers acting ethically, often for no compensation.
Legal Grey Zone
CMA S.3A creates a chilling effect on legitimate security research. Dual-use tools (Metasploit, Nmap, Burp Suite) are used identically by attackers and defenders — the legality hinges on authorisation and intent, not the tool itself. Legal protection for good-faith security research remains inadequate in UK law compared to US Computer Fraud and Abuse Act safe harbours.
Professional Lesson
Security researchers operating in the public interest deserve legal protection. The professional community's commitment to responsible disclosure is a governance mechanism — a self-regulatory system that creates social value. BCS/ACM codes' public interest obligation supports coordinated disclosure as the ethically mandated approach.

MSc-Level Glossary

Every key term — defined with precision.

Accountability (GDPR P7)
The principle requiring data controllers to demonstrate active compliance with all GDPR principles — not merely assert it. Requires policies, DPO appointments, DPIAs, and audit trails.
Actus Reus
The physical or external element of a criminal offence — "the guilty act." Under CMA S.1: accessing computer material without authorisation.
Algorithmic Profiling
The automated analysis of personal data to evaluate, predict, or infer characteristics about individuals — e.g., creditworthiness, health risk, political opinions. Governed by GDPR Art.22.
Anti-Commons Problem
Paradox where excessive IP rights ownership creates so many veto points that further innovation is prevented — the opposite of the intended effect of IP law.
APT (Advanced Persistent Threat)
A sophisticated, long-term, targeted cyber-attack typically conducted by nation-state or state-sponsored actors. Characterised by stealth, persistence, and high capability.
Aspirational Ethics
Ethical conduct that exceeds the minimum legal or regulatory requirement — striving toward the ideals of professional integrity, public service, and excellence.
Biometric Data
Special category data under Art.9 GDPR. Unique physical characteristics used for identification — fingerprints, facial recognition templates, iris scans, voice patterns. Cannot be changed if compromised.
Bug Bounty
A formal programme through which organisations offer financial rewards to security researchers for discovering and responsibly disclosing vulnerabilities. Provides explicit authorisation, removing CMA liability.
CIA Triad
The three pillars of information security: Confidentiality (restricting access to authorised parties), Integrity (ensuring data accuracy and completeness), and Availability (ensuring systems are accessible when needed).
CDPA 1988
Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988. The primary UK statute governing copyright protection for creative works including software source code, object code, and non-literal structural elements.
CMA 1990
Computer Misuse Act 1990. The primary UK statute criminalising unauthorised access to computer systems (S.1), unauthorised modification/impairment (S.3), serious damage (S.3ZA), and supply of hacking tools (S.3A).
Consequentialism
An ethical theory holding that the morality of an action is determined by its consequences. Includes utilitarianism (greatest good) and other outcome-based frameworks.
Data Controller
The entity that determines the purposes and means of processing personal data. Holds primary legal accountability under UK GDPR. Fines up to £17.5M or 4% of global turnover.
Data Minimisation
GDPR Principle 3 — only collect personal data that is adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary for the specified purpose. Core design obligation under Art.25 (Privacy by Design).
Data Processor
An entity that processes personal data on behalf of a Data Controller, acting under instruction. Must operate under a Data Processing Agreement. Now subject to direct ICO enforcement under DPA 2018.
Data Protection Officer (DPO)
Mandatory appointment for large organisations or those processing special category data at scale. Oversees GDPR compliance, acts as ICO contact point, and provides independent advice to the organisation.
Defensible Disposal
The legally documented, auditable process of deleting personal data when retention is no longer justified. Must comply with P5 GDPR and produce a certificate/audit trail demonstrating secure deletion.
Deontology
An ethical theory holding that the morality of an action is determined by adherence to rules or duties, regardless of consequences. Associated with Kant's Categorical Imperative.
Digital Twin
A virtual replica of a physical or digital system used for simulation, testing, and attack rehearsal. Enables red team exercises without affecting live operational systems.
DPA 2018
Data Protection Act 2018. The UK Act implementing and supplementing the UK GDPR. Grants the ICO enforcement powers including fines up to £17.5M or 4% of global annual turnover.
Endpoint Monitoring
Surveillance conducted on a specific device — keystroke logging, screen recording, application monitoring. Higher privacy risk than network monitoring; requires stronger justification.
GDPR (UK GDPR)
General Data Protection Regulation as retained in UK law post-Brexit. Establishes seven data processing principles, data subject rights, and controller/processor obligations. Enforced by the ICO.
HUHT / HULT / LUHT / LULT
Red team threat actor classification framework: High/Low Utility (capability) × High/Low Threat (motivation to target you). Used to prioritise defensive investment and red team simulation scenarios.
ICO
Information Commissioner's Office. UK's independent data protection regulator. Can issue fines up to £17.5M or 4% of global turnover, conduct audits, impose enforcement notices, and prosecute criminal DPA offences.
Infopaq Standard
The EU originality test for copyright established in Infopaq v Danske (2009): a work attracts copyright protection if it is the "author's own intellectual creation" — even as few as 11 words.
Information Lifecycle Management (ILM)
The governance framework managing data through its entire lifecycle: Collect → Store → Use → Archive → Dispose. Each stage carries specific legal obligations and security controls.
Lawful Basis
One of six grounds under GDPR Art.6 that makes data processing lawful: consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, or legitimate interests.
Legitimate Interests (LIA)
Lawful basis for processing requiring a three-part test: (1) identify a genuine purpose; (2) demonstrate processing is necessary; (3) balance against individuals' rights and interests.
Mens Rea
The mental element of a criminal offence — the "guilty mind." Under CMA S.1: intentionally accessing a system, knowing there is no authorisation. Absent mens rea = no criminal offence.
Network Monitoring
Surveillance of data in transit across an organisation's infrastructure — web logs, bandwidth monitoring, packet analysis. Governed by RIPA if intercepting live communications.
NIST 800-115
NIST Technical Guide to Information Security Testing and Assessment. The framework structuring authorised penetration testing: Planning → Discovery → Attack → Reporting.
OSINT
Open Source Intelligence — aggregating publicly available information to build intelligence profiles. Raises the aggregation problem: individually innocuous data, combined, may constitute a privacy invasion even if each element is publicly available.
Passing Off
A UK tort (unregistered trade mark equivalent) — misrepresenting one's goods/services as those of another, thereby trading on their reputation and goodwill. Protects unregistered brand identity.
Penetration Testing
Authorised, structured testing of computer systems using attacker techniques to identify security vulnerabilities. Requires written authorisation (Letter of Authorisation) to be exempt from CMA offences.
Privacy by Design
GDPR Art.25 obligation to embed data protection considerations into system design from the outset — not as an afterthought. Requires data minimisation, access controls, and privacy-protective default settings.
Proportionality
A principle requiring that monitoring, data collection, or other intrusive measures be the minimum necessary to achieve a legitimate purpose. Failure of proportionality renders otherwise lawful monitoring unlawful.
Purpose Limitation
GDPR Principle 2 — personal data collected for a specified, explicit, legitimate purpose cannot be further processed for incompatible purposes. Core constraint on data reuse and AI training.
Ransomware
Malware that encrypts a victim's data and demands payment for the decryption key. A CMA S.3 offence (impairment). Typically also engages S.3A (supply/creation of malicious tool).
Reasonableness Standard
From Blyth v Birmingham Waterworks (1856): conduct is assessed against what a prudent, reasonable person would have done. Applied to professional negligence, AI liability, and computing ethics.
Red Teaming
Adversarial simulation of attacks against one's own systems, processes, and people. Tests systemic defences by role-playing as a hostile actor. Broader than penetration testing — evaluates people and culture, not just technology.
RIPA 2000
Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000. Makes interception of live communications unlawful without explicit consent or business necessity. TLBP Regulations 2000 create a narrow business purpose exception.
Special Category Data
GDPR Art.9 — sensitive personal data requiring elevated protection: health, genetic, biometric, racial/ethnic, political, religious, sexual orientation data. Processing requires explicit consent or specific legal basis.
Supply Chain Attack
An attack targeting a less-secure element in a target's supply chain (software dependencies, hardware vendors, service providers) to gain access to the primary target. Bybit and SolarWinds are canonical examples.
Surveillance Capitalism
Shoshana Zuboff's concept describing an economic system in which personal data is the raw material for predicting and modifying human behaviour — primarily for advertising. Challenges the GDPR consent model structurally.
TLBP Regulations 2000
Telecommunications (Lawful Business Practice) Regulations 2000. Creates a business-purpose exception to RIPA allowing employer monitoring of communications without consent, provided: legitimate purpose, all-reasonable-steps to inform staff, and proportionality.
Virtue Ethics
An ethical theory centred on the moral character of the agent rather than rules or outcomes. From Aristotle. Key virtues in computing: integrity, honesty, moral courage, prudence. Applied to: whistleblowing, professional resilience, ethical leadership.

Final Distinction Insights

The biggest ideas — and why they will define your professional lifetime.

The Five Biggest Themes Across This Module
1
Responsible Power
Computing professionals exercise unprecedented societal power — over elections (algorithmic curation), health (AI diagnostics), finance (automated trading), and privacy (mass surveillance). The core lesson of this module is that power must be matched by proportionate responsibility. Every framework studied — GDPR, CMA, BCS code, ACAS — is an attempt to institutionalise that responsibility. The professional who understands this operates at a fundamentally different level than one who treats these as compliance checklists.
2
The Inadequacy of Law Alone
Every legal framework studied is in some respect inadequate: the CMA was written before AI; GDPR's consent model does not survive contact with surveillance capitalism; intellectual property law has not resolved AI authorship; employment law was not designed for algorithmic management. The professional who relies solely on legal compliance will always be one step behind. Ethics and governance exist precisely to fill these gaps — and to anticipate the next legal failure before it produces the next Post Office scandal.
3
Accountability Cannot Be Automated
The Post Office, NHS DeepMind, Horizon, and every governance failure studied share a common thread: the diffusion of accountability across organisations, technologies, and hierarchies until no single person or entity could be held responsible. Governance frameworks are only as effective as the humans who operate them with genuine accountability. The most important professional skill this module teaches is not technical — it is the ability and willingness to say "I am responsible" and act accordingly.
4
Trust Is the Foundation of Digital Society
Every system studied depends on trust: GDPR is a trust framework for data use; the CMA is a trust framework for computer access; professional codes are trust frameworks for practitioner conduct. When trust breaks — when Facebook lies about safety research, when the Post Office falsely prosecutes innocent people, when TikTok secretly collects behavioural data — it causes harm far beyond the individual breach. Rebuilding trust is exponentially harder than maintaining it.
5
The Ethical Technologist Is the Future
The integration of AI into every domain of life creates a structural demand for professionals who can navigate the intersection of technical capability, legal constraint, and ethical obligation. The organisations that will thrive in the next decade are those with professionals capable of asking "should we?" as well as "can we?" — and the courage to act on the answer. This is the graduate that this module is designed to produce.
Future of AI Governance — The Next Decade

EU AI Act (2024–2027)

First comprehensive AI regulation globally. Risk-tiered approach: unacceptable risk (banned) → high risk (strict conformity assessment) → limited risk (transparency obligations) → minimal risk (voluntary codes). Will shape global AI governance norms as GDPR shaped global data protection.

AI Liability Frameworks

The EU AI Liability Directive will introduce fault-based and strict liability regimes for AI harms. The Blyth v Birmingham reasonableness standard will be central to fault assessment — what would a reasonable, competent AI developer have done?

Algorithmic Transparency

Growing regulatory demand for explainability in AI decisions — particularly in high-stakes domains (credit, employment, healthcare). GDPR Art.22 right to explanation is the legal foundation; technical XAI (Explainable AI) is the engineering challenge.

Cyber Warfare Norms

The Tallinn Manual attempts to apply international humanitarian law to cyber operations, but state practice remains contested. The Bybit heist demonstrates that attribution and enforcement against nation-state actors remains practically impossible — requiring governance solutions above and beyond domestic law.

AI Training Data Rights

The resolution of Getty Images v Stability AI and NYT v OpenAI will determine whether the current AI development model is legally sustainable. The outcome will reshape IP law, GDPR training data obligations, and the economics of AI development simultaneously.

Professional Certification for AI

Growing pressure for formal ethical certification requirements for AI practitioners — equivalent to the chartered engineer model. The BCS/ACM codes provide the ethical foundation; formal licensing structures for high-risk AI deployment may follow the EU AI Act's lead.
